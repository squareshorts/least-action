\begin{abstract}
Cognitive conflict is often inferred from response time, accuracy, or trajectory curvature, but these summaries do not specify the latent geometry of an unfolding decision. This study formalizes semantic response competition as stochastic movement through an action landscape. A public mouse-tracking categorization dataset was reanalyzed after mapping all correct trials to a common two-dimensional response space. The primary model treated the minimum-jerk target trajectory as a motor template and estimated a competitor-attraction parameter, $\rho$, from least-action deformations of that template. In subject-wise cross-validation, the action model predicted trajectories better than a minimum-jerk baseline, especially for atypical items. Trial-level $\rho$ was higher for atypical than typical trials and predicted curvature beyond condition and subject effects. Item-level semantic margins were computed from a transparent repository scoring table before stochastic trajectory likelihood evaluation. Under leave-one-item-out stochastic path likelihood, the semantic-margin action model outperformed a condition-only action model: 15 of 19 items showed lower NLL, with a mean item-wise gain of 0.694 NLL units. Sensitivity, permutation, and mixed-effects checks evaluate whether this result survives changes in fixed action parameters, shuffled item semantics, and subject/item grouping structure. The results support a constrained action-landscape account of response competition, while also identifying the semantic-score provenance and secondary-predictor validation as important targets for prospective replication.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} mouse tracking; semantic categorization; least action; cognitive conflict; potential landscape; stochastic action model; response competition

\section{Introduction}

Human categorization unfolds over time. Partial evidence, competing categories, and motor preparation interact before the final response is registered. Mouse-tracking experiments capture this temporal structure by recording the trajectory of the hand as participants move toward a response. When a cursor is pulled toward an unchosen alternative, the movement can reveal competition that is not fully expressed in accuracy or response time \citep{spivey2005continuous,freeman2010mousetracker,dale2007graded}. Such effects motivate dynamic accounts of categorization in which cognition is treated as a temporally extended process rather than as a sequence of discrete symbolic stages.

This study models dynamic response competition using a least-action formalism. A decision trajectory is treated as a stochastic path shaped by motor smoothness constraints and by attraction toward a competing response. The approach belongs to a broader family of variational and optimal-control models in cognitive science, including free-energy and optimal-feedback-control accounts \citep{friston2010free,todorov2002optimal}. The present model applies this logic to a concrete behavioral phenomenon: semantic response competition in mouse-tracking categorization.

The dataset analyzed here is the KH2017 mouse-tracking dataset distributed with the \texttt{mousetrap} ecosystem \citep{kieslich2017mousetrap,kh2017raw}. Participants categorized animal exemplars in a two-choice task. Some items were typical category members, whereas others were atypical and elicited competition from a salient but incorrect category. This task structure is ideal for testing whether item-level category structure deforms an action landscape.

The analysis had two stages. First, an action model was fit to canonicalized trajectories, and the inferred competitor-attraction parameter $\rho$ was tested as a cognitive-conflict variable. Second, item-level semantic margins were computed from the repository semantic-score table, before leave-one-item-out likelihood evaluation, and used to predict $\rho$ for held-out items. The critical test was whether the semantic-prior action model improved held-out stochastic trajectory NLL relative to a condition-only action model.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Dataset and preprocessing}

The analysis used the public KH2017 mouse-tracking dataset \citep{kh2017raw}. The raw dataset contained 1,140 rows. After preprocessing and restriction to correct canonicalized trials, the final trajectory dataset contained 1,064 trials from 60 participants. The item-level semantic analysis included 19 animal exemplars. The dataset included time-stamped mouse coordinates for each trial, together with subject, exemplar, condition, response, and response-time information. Table~\ref{tab:dataset} summarizes the analysis sample.

\input{outputs/tables/table_dataset.tex}

The decision geometry was canonicalized so that all trials shared a common coordinate system. The start point was mapped to the lower center of the response space, the target response to the upper right, and the competitor response to the upper left. Trajectories were time-normalized to a common number of samples for model fitting and likelihood evaluation.

\subsection{Primary action-landscape model}

Each cursor trajectory was represented as a path $q(t)=(x(t),y(t))$ over normalized time. The implemented primary model is a nested action model around a minimum-jerk motor template $q_M(t)$ \citep{flash1985coordination}. For a candidate path $q$, let $d(t)=q(t)-q_M(t)$ be the deformation away from the direct target-directed motor plan. The discrete action minimized for a fixed $\rho$ was
\begin{equation}
S_K(q;\rho)=
\Delta t \sum_{k=0}^{K-1}
\left[
\frac{\alpha}{2}\|d_k\|^2+
\frac{\beta}{2}\|\ddot d_k\|^2
- \lambda \rho (1-t_k)^\gamma
\exp\left(-\frac{\|q_k-r_C\|^2}{2\sigma_C^2}\right)
\right] + B(q),
\end{equation}
where $r_C=(-1,1)$ is the competitor response, $\lambda$ is the potential scale, and $\gamma$ controls decay of competitor attraction over normalized time. The boundary term $B(q)$ is an indicator barrier implemented by box constraints: it is 0 for internal points with $x\in[-1.45,1.45]$ and $y\in[-0.15,1.25]$, and $+\infty$ outside this feasible region. When $\rho=0$, the primary action model reduces to the target-directed minimum-jerk template plus deformation penalties.

This implemented primary model differs from the more general target-plus-competitor Gaussian potential sometimes used in action-landscape notation. The repository also contains a physical target-plus-competitor ablation, but the manuscript's predictive and semantic-prior results use the nested primary model described above. Table~\ref{tab:numerical-settings} lists the exact numerical settings extracted from the code and centralized in \texttt{config/model\_config.yaml}.

\subsection{Baselines and model hierarchy}

The first motor baseline was a minimum-jerk trajectory from the start point to the target \citep{flash1985coordination}. Flexible non-Lagrangian baselines were also evaluated: condition-level Bezier attraction curves, spline attraction curves, and condition-mean trajectories. These baselines directly summarize trajectory shape. The action model instead specifies a response-attractor mechanism and yields the interpretable latent parameter $\rho$.

The model hierarchy also included physical action ablations: motor only, target only, shared target-plus-competitor attraction, condition-specific target-plus-competitor attraction, and trial-level upper bounds. These ablations are reported to locate the primary model relative to less semantic or less constrained alternatives.

\subsection{Semantic-score transparency}

Semantic scores were generated by \path{scripts/compute_semantic_scores.py} and saved to \path{outputs/semantic_scores_19_items.csv}. The repository does not contain a machine-readable published norm file for these 19 items, so the manuscript does not cite an unsupported named norm source for these values. Instead, the semantic scores are treated as a transparent repository data file \citep{semantic_scores_data}. For each item, the table stores a target-category score, \texttt{typ\_target}, and a competitor-category score, \texttt{typ\_competitor}, on a 1--7 scale where smaller values mean stronger category typicality. The similarity scores were computed exactly as
\begin{align}
\mathrm{sim}_{T,i} &= 1 / \mathrm{typ}_{T,i},\\
\mathrm{sim}_{C,i} &= 1 / \mathrm{typ}_{C,i},\\
\mathrm{margin}_i &= \mathrm{sim}_{T,i} - \mathrm{sim}_{C,i}.
\end{align}
A lower or negative margin indicates greater competitor similarity. Table~\ref{tab:semantic-scores} reports the full semantic table used in the analyses.

\input{outputs/tables/table_semantic_scores.tex}

\subsection{Stochastic action likelihood}

Model comparison used a Gaussian path-deviation likelihood. For model $m$, the observed trajectory was modeled as
\begin{equation}
q_{\mathrm{obs}}(t)=q^*_m(t)+\epsilon(t), \qquad
\epsilon(t)\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_m^2 I).
\end{equation}
For a trajectory with $K$ two-dimensional samples, NLL was computed as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{NLL}_{im}
=
K\log(2\pi\sigma_m^2)
+
\frac{1}{2\sigma_m^2}
\sum_{k=0}^{K-1}
\left\|q_{i,\mathrm{obs}}(t_k)-q^*_{m}(t_k)\right\|^2 .
\end{equation}
The variance parameter was estimated only from training data in the corresponding fold. Lower NLL indicates better out-of-sample stochastic trajectory prediction.

\subsection{Cross-validation and validation analyses}

Subject-wise cross-validation evaluated whether the action model generalized across participants relative to the minimum-jerk baseline. Leave-one-item-out cross-validation evaluated whether semantic margins generalized to unseen exemplars. In each leave-one-item-out fold, every trajectory belonging to one exemplar was held out, model parameters and path variances were estimated from the remaining exemplars, and NLL was evaluated on the held-out exemplar.

Three additional validation analyses were added for submission. First, fixed action parameters were perturbed one factor at a time across changes in $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\sigma_C$, and $K$. Second, semantic margins were randomly permuted across items 5,000 times, and the leave-one-item-out semantic-prior NLL gain was recomputed for each permutation. Third, a mixed-effects validation tested whether semantic margin predicted trial-level $\rho$ beyond condition with random subject and item structure when estimable in Python.

\subsection{Sensitivity, permutation, and mixed-effects validation}

The semantic-prior result was evaluated under one-factor perturbations of the fixed action settings. Across these settings, the margin--$\rho$ association remained negative, and the semantic-prior NLL gain remained positive in the baseline setting and in most nearby perturbations. These checks indicate that the semantic-prior effect is not an artifact of one arbitrary numerical setting, although the exact NLL gain changes with the fixed action geometry.

\section{Discussion}

The results support a constrained semantic action-landscape account of response competition. An action model with a competitor-attraction term predicted mouse trajectories better than a minimum-jerk motor baseline, with larger improvements for atypical items. The fitted competitor-attraction parameter $\rho$ recovered the experimental manipulation, predicted trajectory deviation and response time beyond condition and subject effects, and tracked item-level error and response-time difficulty. Item-level semantic margins predicted $\rho$ and improved held-out stochastic trajectory NLL relative to a condition-only action model.

The comparison with flexible descriptive baselines clarifies the role of the model. Condition means, Bezier curves, and splines can approximate observed trajectory shape directly. They are useful trajectory predictors, and some outperform the constrained action model in raw RMSE and NLL. The action model's contribution is interpretability: it maps item-level semantic structure onto a latent landscape parameter, maps that parameter onto path probability, and maps the resulting path geometry onto behavioral difficulty.

The semantic-score provenance is also a limitation. The present repository contains a transparent semantic table and an exact transform, but it does not contain a directly verifiable published norm file for these 19 item-category pairs. This is an internal derived semantic scoring table and therefore the semantic-prior result should be treated as transparent retrospective validation, not definitive external-norm validation. A secondary independent semantic predictor was not added because the repository lacks an embedding table, a second norm source, or a frozen LLM rating table; this absence is reported in \path{outputs/secondary_semantic_predictor.csv} rather than filled with invented values.

The strongest next test is prospective or semi-prospective validation with new items. A direct next validation is to freeze either a published norm-derived margin or an embedding-derived margin before fitting trajectories in a new item set. A new set of 30--60 animal exemplars should be selected to span semantic margin before trajectory analysis. Semantic margins should be precomputed and frozen, then item-level $\rho$, response time, error rate, and AUC should be predicted in preregistered order. New trajectories, or a strictly held-out trajectory subset, can then test whether predicted $\rho$ and behavioral difficulty follow that order. This design would separate semantic prediction from trajectory fitting more cleanly than the present 19-item retrospective analysis.

Several limitations qualify the interpretation. First, the item-level semantic analysis used only 19 exemplars, so item-level regressions and leave-one-item-out likelihood differences should be treated as evidence for model viability rather than as a definitive population-level estimate. Second, the primary action model is intentionally constrained and does not beat all flexible trajectory baselines. Third, the current semantic predictor is transparent but not yet independently replicated. These limitations motivate a prospective stimulus set, independent semantic norms or embeddings, and hierarchical modeling of subject and item variation. Accordingly, the present results should be viewed as a reproducible model-based reanalysis that motivates prospective semantic prediction, rather than as a definitive population-level estimate of semantic-action coupling.

\section{Conclusion}

Semantic response competition in mouse-tracking categorization can be formalized as deformation of an action landscape. The competitor-attraction parameter $\rho$ is higher for atypical items, predicts movement deviation and response time, correlates with item-level difficulty, and is recoverable in simulation. Semantic margins computed before likelihood evaluation predict $\rho$ and improve leave-one-item-out stochastic trajectory NLL relative to a condition-only action model. These findings support a mechanistically interpretable variational framework for dynamic response competition, while identifying semantic-source provenance and prospective validation as the key next reproducibility steps.
