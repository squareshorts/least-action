\begin{abstract}
Mouse tracking reveals response competition during categorization, but standard measures such as response time, accuracy, area under the curve, and maximum deviation do not specify the latent geometry that generates a curved response path. This study introduces a stochastic least-action model that treats each cursor trajectory as a noisy realization of an action path shaped by motor smoothness and attraction toward a competing response. The model estimates a competitor-attraction parameter, $\rho$, which quantifies how strongly the unchosen response deforms the movement toward the chosen target.

The model was evaluated in a public German semantic-categorization mouse-tracking dataset after mapping correct trials to a common two-dimensional response geometry. In subject-wise cross-validation, the action model outperformed a minimum-jerk motor baseline, with the largest gain for atypical items. Trial-level $\rho$ was higher for atypical than typical trials and predicted trajectory curvature beyond condition and subject effects. At the item level, semantic target--competitor margins predicted the fitted $\rho$ and improved leave-one-item-out stochastic path likelihood relative to a condition-only action model: 15 of 19 items showed a lower NLL, with a mean item-wise gain of 0.694 NLL units. Path models linked lower semantic margins to a larger AUC and maximum deviation through higher $\rho$. Controls ruled out condition label, word length, permuted margins, and random competitor assignment; item-jackknife slopes remained negative. Semantic inference was conducted at the exemplar level, avoiding trial-level inflation of an item-level predictor. A multisource embedding validation converged with the main analysis, and the same fitted parameter distinguished lookalike from ordinary objects in an independent visual mouse-tracking dataset.

Together, these results show that semantic and perceptual category structure can be estimated from the way competing representations deform action. The model turns mouse trajectories from descriptive traces of response conflict into stochastic measurements of latent competitor attraction.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} mouse tracking; semantic categorization; least action; cognitive conflict; stochastic action model; response competition

\section{Introduction}

Categorical decisions enter most analyses as discrete endpoint labels, but mouse-tracking work shows that the route to the endpoint contains information about the unfolding decision. The response time constrains how long the selection took, whereas the cursor trajectories preserve continuous spatial evidence about partial commitment, correction, and attraction to unchosen alternatives \citep{schoemann2021mouse,meyer2024mouse}. In categorization tasks, this makes the hand path a process-level record of response competition rather than a secondary motor artifact.

Standard behavioral summaries compress this process. Accuracy records the winning response, response time records the duration of selection, and descriptive trajectory measures such as area under the curve or maximum deviation quantify the resulting curvature. These measures establish that a response path was deformed, but they do not estimate the latent attraction that produced the deformation. The present study addresses that gap by modeling mouse trajectories as stochastic paths through an action landscape.

The model uses a target-directed minimum-jerk trajectory as the motor template and adds a time-decaying potential toward the competing response. The fitted competitor-attraction parameter, $\rho$, is the amount of attraction required for the action model to reproduce the observed cursor path. In this framework, $\rho$ is not a renamed curvature statistic: AUC and maximum deviation describe the path, whereas $\rho$ estimates the latent action-landscape parameter that generated the deformation.

The empirical analysis uses the public KH2017 German semantic-categorization mouse-tracking dataset distributed with the \texttt{mousetrap} ecosystem \citep{kieslich2017mousetrap,kh2017raw}. Participants categorized animal exemplars in a two-choice task containing typical items and atypical items that elicited competition from a salient incorrect category. The model is evaluated by testing whether $\rho$ generalizes across subjects, recovers the typical--atypical manipulation, predicts movement deformation beyond condition labels, and varies systematically with item-level semantic structure. The central item-level prediction is that smaller target--competitor semantic margins produce larger fitted $\rho$ and better held-out stochastic path likelihood than a condition-only action model.

A multisource embedding analysis and an independent visual mouse-tracking validation test the scope of the semantic--action link. If the same fitted parameter is predicted by computational semantic margins and also distinguishes lookalike from ordinary objects in visual categorization, then $\rho$ captures a general form of response competition: competing meanings and percepts deform the action used to express the selected response.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Dataset and preprocessing}

The analysis used the public KH2017 mouse-tracking dataset \citep{kh2017raw}. The raw dataset contained 1,140 rows. After preprocessing and restriction to correct canonicalized trials, the final trajectory dataset contained 1,064 trials from 60 participants. The item-level semantic analysis included 19 animal exemplars. The dataset included time-stamped mouse coordinates for each trial, together with subject, exemplar, condition, response, and response-time information. Table~\ref{tab:dataset} summarizes the analysis sample.

\input{tables/table_dataset.tex}

The decision geometry was canonicalized so that all trials shared a common coordinate system. The start point was mapped to the lower center of the response space, the target response to the upper right, and the competitor response to the upper left. Trajectories were time-normalized to a common number of samples for model fitting and likelihood evaluation.

\subsection{Primary action-landscape model}

Each cursor trajectory was represented as a path $q(t)=(x(t),y(t))$ over normalized time. The implemented primary model is a nested action model around a minimum-jerk motor template $q_M(t)$ \citep{flash1985coordination}. For a candidate path $q$, let $d(t)=q(t)-q_M(t)$ be the deformation away from the direct target-directed motor plan. The discrete action minimized for a fixed $\rho$ was
\begin{equation}
S_K(q;\rho)=
\Delta t \sum_{k=0}^{K-1}
\left[
\frac{\alpha}{2}\|d_k\|^2+
\frac{\beta}{2}\|\ddot d_k\|^2
- \lambda \rho (1-t_k)^\gamma
\exp\left(-\frac{\|q_k-r_C\|^2}{2\sigma_C^2}\right)
\right] + B(q).
\label{eq:primary-action}
\end{equation}
where $r_C=(-1,1)$ is the competitor response, $\lambda$ is the potential scale, and $\gamma$ controls decay of competitor attraction over normalized time. The boundary term $B(q)$ is an indicator barrier implemented by box constraints: it is 0 for internal points with $x\in[-1.45,1.45]$ and $y\in[-0.15,1.25]$, and $+\infty$ outside this feasible region. When $\rho=0$, the primary action model reduces to the target-directed minimum-jerk template plus deformation penalties.

The fitted value of $\rho$ is a model-based estimate of the competitor attraction required to deform a target-directed motor path into the observed cursor path. AUC and maximum deviation describe the geometry of the path; $\rho$ estimates the latent action-landscape parameter that generates that geometry and can be predicted from semantic structure under held-out likelihood.

Predictive and semantic-prior analyses use the nested primary model in Equation~\ref{eq:primary-action}. Physical target-plus-competitor ablations are reported as model-comparison checks. Table~\ref{tab:numerical-settings} lists the numerical settings used for the primary model.

\subsection{Baselines and model hierarchy}

The first motor baseline was a minimum-jerk trajectory from the start point to the target \citep{flash1985coordination}. Flexible non-Lagrangian baselines were also evaluated: condition-level Bezier attraction curves, spline attraction curves, and condition-mean trajectories. These baselines directly summarize trajectory shape. The action model instead specifies a response-attractor mechanism and yields the interpretable latent parameter $\rho$.

The model hierarchy also included physical action ablations: motor only, target only, shared target-plus-competitor attraction, condition-specific target-plus-competitor attraction, and trial-level upper bounds.

\subsection{Semantic margins}

For each exemplar, the semantic predictor was defined as an inverse-typicality margin between the correct target category and the salient competitor category, following the prototype-theoretic view that category membership is graded rather than purely discrete \citep{rosch1975family}. Each item was assigned a target-category typicality score, \texttt{typ\_target}, and a competitor-category typicality score, \texttt{typ\_competitor}, on a scale of 1--7, where smaller values indicate stronger category typicality. These item-level scores are explicitly reported in Table~\ref{tab:semantic-scores}. The target and competitor typicality values were transformed into similarity scores and then into a semantic margin:
\begin{align}
\mathrm{sim}_{T,i} &= 1 / \mathrm{typ}_{T,i},\\
\mathrm{sim}_{C,i} &= 1 / \mathrm{typ}_{C,i},\\
\mathrm{margin}_i &= \mathrm{sim}_{T,i} - \mathrm{sim}_{C,i}.
\end{align}
Lower margins indicate greater relative similarity to the competitor category and therefore predict stronger competitor attraction in the action model. A multisource computational semantic analysis tested whether this relation also appeared in transformer-based semantic representations (Section~\ref{sec:multisource-margin}).

\subsection{Multisource computational semantic validation}
\label{sec:multisource-margin}

A multisource computational semantic analysis tested whether the semantic--action relation generalized beyond the item--category scoring table. The validation used three pretrained multilingual sentence-embedding models: \texttt{T-Systems-onsite/cross-en-de-roberta-sentence-transformer}, \texttt{paraphrase-multilingual-mpnet-base-v2}, and \texttt{intfloat/multilingual-e5-base}. These models are based on sentence-transformer and transformer-embedding architectures, including SBERT, RoBERTa, MPNet, and multilingual E5 \citep{reimers2019sentence,liu2019roberta,song2020mpnet,wang2024multilingual}.

Before embedding extraction, transliterated German forms were converted to canonical orthography, for example, \textit{Saeugetier} $\rightarrow$ \textit{Säugetier}, \textit{Chamaeleon} $\rightarrow$ \textit{Chamäleon}, and \textit{Seeloewe} $\rightarrow$ \textit{Seelöwe}. For each model, item names and category labels were encoded in German. Cosine similarities $\mathrm{emb\_sim}_{T,i}$ and $\mathrm{emb\_sim}_{C,i}$ were computed between each item and its target and competitor categories. The embedding margin was defined as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{emb\_margin}_i =
\mathrm{emb\_sim}_{T,i}
-
\mathrm{emb\_sim}_{C,i}.
\end{equation}

Lower embedding margins indicate greater relative proximity to the competitor category. A rank-averaged multisource margin was computed by averaging within-source ranks across the three embedding models.

\subsection{Stochastic action likelihood}

Model comparison used a Gaussian path-deviation likelihood. For model $m$, the observed trajectory was modeled as
\begin{equation}
q_{\mathrm{obs}}(t)=q^*_m(t)+\epsilon(t), \qquad
\epsilon(t)\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_m^2 I).
\end{equation}
For a trajectory with $K$ two-dimensional samples, NLL was computed as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{NLL}_{im}
=
K\log(2\pi\sigma_m^2)
+
\frac{1}{2\sigma_m^2}
\sum_{k=0}^{K-1}
\left\|q_{i,\mathrm{obs}}(t_k)-q^*_{m}(t_k)\right\|^2 .
\end{equation}
The variance parameter was estimated only from training data in the corresponding fold. Lower NLL indicates better out-of-sample stochastic trajectory prediction.

\subsection{Cross-validation and validation analyses}

Subject-wise cross-validation tested whether the action model generalized across participants relative to the minimum-jerk baseline. Leave-one-item-out cross-validation tested whether semantic margins generalized to unseen exemplars. In each leave-one-item-out fold, all trajectories belonging to one exemplar were held out, model parameters and path variances were estimated from the remaining exemplars, and NLL was evaluated on the held-out exemplar.

Semantic-margin inference was conducted at the item level because the semantic predictor varies by exemplar, not by trial. Trial-level analyses were used to estimate trajectories, validate subject-level generalization, and test whether fitted $\rho$ predicted movement deformation beyond condition and subject effects. Item-level semantic claims were evaluated with leave-one-item-out prediction, item-resampling bootstrap intervals, item-permutation tests, and leave-one-item-out influence checks. This analysis structure prevents trial-level pseudoreplication: the semantic-margin effect is tested across exemplars, whereas the trial-level data support trajectory estimation and mixed-effects validation.

\subsection{Path, control, and item-influence analyses}

The central model chain was tested by linking semantic margin, fitted competitor attraction, and behavioral difficulty. At the item level, lower semantic margins predicted higher $\rho$, and higher $\rho$ predicted larger AUC and maximum deviation. For AUC and maximum deviation, the indirect paths through $\rho$ were negative and their bootstrap confidence intervals excluded zero (indirect effects $=-0.172$ and $=-0.488$, respectively). The direct margin effects were close to zero after $\rho$ was included. Response time and error showed weaker indirect paths, indicating that $\rho$ primarily captures the movement-deformation component of semantic competition.

\input{tables/table_path_mediation_item.tex}

Targeted controls tested whether semantic structure contributed beyond condition labels and lexical form. The real target--competitor margin improved NLL over the condition-only action model and exceeded word length, permuted margins, and random competitor-score assignment. Competitor similarity alone was also strong, indicating that proximity to the competing response category is the active semantic component.

The item-influence analysis quantified the stability of the semantic-margin slope. The full margin--$\rho$ slope was $-0.546$, with a bootstrap 95\% CI of $[-0.699,-0.362]$. The Spearman association was $r_s=-0.855$, with a bootstrap 95\% CI of $[-0.969,-0.570]$, and the Theil--Sen slope was $-0.482$ with a 95\% CI of $[-0.652,-0.342]$. Removing each exemplar in turn preserved the negative slope, with jackknife slopes ranging from $-0.571$ to $-0.439$. \subsection{Held-out stochastic likelihood}

The primary semantic-prior test compared action models under leave-one-item-out stochastic path likelihood. The semantic-margin-only action model outperformed the condition-only action model in item-weighted NLL. Mean item-level NLL was 39.864 for the condition-only model and 39.171 for the semantic-margin model, a mean item-wise gain of 0.694 NLL units. The gain was positive for 15 of 19 items (exact two-tailed sign test against $H_0: p = 0.5$, $p = 0.019$). The 95\% bootstrap confidence interval for the mean item-wise gain, obtained by item-resampling (4,000 replications), was [0.230, 1.384] NLL units. Figure~\ref{fig:semantic-priors} summarizes the semantic-prior diagnostics: the fitted semantic--$\rho$ relation, leave-one-item-out prediction of $\rho$, held-out stochastic NLL, and behavioral difficulty associations. \subsection{Item difficulty and parameter recovery}

Item-level $\rho$ tracked independent difficulty measures, and simulation-based recovery showed that the grid-search procedure can identify the generating $\rho$ under the noise levels tested. Approximately 7.0\% of trial-level fits were at $\rho = 0.00$ and 3.4\% at $\rho = 2.00$; no item-level mean fit reached either boundary. Error-trial analysis confirmed that lower semantic margins predicted higher trial-level error rates ($\hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{margin}} = -5.71$ in logistic regression), consistent with semantic competition affecting both trajectory curvature and accuracy.

\section{Discussion}

This study introduces a stochastic action-landscape model for estimating response competition from mouse trajectories. The model decomposes a curved response path into a target-directed motor template and a fitted competitor-attraction parameter. Under this formulation, semantic category structure predicts the latent action-landscape parameter that deforms the response path.

The fitted parameter $\rho$ behaved as a process variable. It was higher for atypical than typical trials, predicted AUC and maximum deviation beyond condition and subject effects, tracked item-level response difficulty, and was recoverable in simulation. These properties separate $\rho$ from a post hoc curvature measure: it is the competitor attraction required for the stochastic action model to reproduce the observed movement.

The semantic analysis uses exemplar identity as the inferential unit because semantic margin is an item-level predictor. The relevant sample size for semantic generalization is therefore the 19-item set, not the 1,064 trial trajectories. The manuscript treats this directly rather than inflating item-level evidence with trial-level counts: semantic effects are evaluated with leave-one-item-out prediction, item-resampling bootstrap intervals, item-permutation tests, and leave-one-item-out influence checks. The trial-level data provide trajectory estimation, subject-level cross-validation, and mixed-effects validation; the item-level analyses test whether category structure predicts fitted competitor attraction across exemplars.

The control analyses reinforce this interpretation. Condition label, word length, permuted margins, and random competitor-score assignments did not reproduce the semantic effect. Leave-one-item-out influence checks preserved the negative semantic-margin slope after each exemplar was removed in turn. Competitor similarity alone was informative, consistent with the role of the competitor potential in the action landscape.

The model also clarifies the relation between prediction and interpretation. Flexible descriptive baselines, such as condition means, Bezier curves, and splines, can directly approximate empirical trajectory shape. The action model serves a different purpose: it maps semantic structure onto a latent landscape parameter, maps that parameter onto stochastic path probability, and links the resulting path geometry to behavioral difficulty. This is the methodological contribution.

\section{Conclusion}

Mouse trajectories can be used to estimate how representational competition becomes action. The stochastic least-action model introduced here measures competitor attraction with $\rho$, a latent parameter that is higher for atypical items, predicts movement deformation, tracks item-level difficulty, and is recoverable in simulation. Semantic margins predict $\rho$ and improve leave-one-item-out stochastic trajectory likelihood beyond condition labels. Multisource semantic validation and an independent visual mouse-tracking dataset converge on the same interpretation: competing meanings and percepts deform the action path before the final response is completed. The result is a method for turning mouse-tracking trajectories into stochastic measurements of latent response competition.
